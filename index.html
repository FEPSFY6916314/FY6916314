Abstract


Rotating detonation engines (RDEs) are a type of rocket engine which relies on detonative combustion rather than conventional deflagrative combustion. It does not follow the design parameter you assign to rocket engines. However, because of its design and increased efficiency, RDEs present a cleaner and cheaper form of space transportation. This work is designed to be an overview of RDEs with some look into induvial research.

Introduction


RDEs, as a concept, promise greater fuel efficiency and thrust output than conventional rocket engines.(Shen  et al, 2022) This is important, as most of the aerospace sector relies on inefficient engines, which cannot harness the full potential energy output of the propellants, and the industry continues expand due to military (Oyewole and Olumba, 2024), business (Gerbis, 2023) and tinkers. They have recently taken on more commercial interest as Venus Aerospace, is a pioneer as the first company to launch a rocket powered by an RDE on 14th May 2025 09:37 EDT (Dinner, 2025).

Analysis and discussion

Operation Principles of RDEs


RDEs operate on detonation combustion over conventional deflagration combustion. The difference is that for detonation reactions, the reaction happens at supersonic speeds, causing shock waves, so the product travels in the same direction as the reaction, whereas in deflagration, the rate of burning is subsonic and the principle is like a candle; the product travels in the opposite direction to the burn and occurs layer by layer. (Venugopalan, 2015). So, in the case of RDEs, these shockwaves, produced by combustion, rotate in an annular (ring shape) chamber with it blasting out the nozzle, and are constantly being fed propellants to maintain the shockwave. 



 Previous experiments on RDEs




Case 1: Small Hydrogen Oxygen RDE (ARMBRUSTER et al., 2023)

An RDE was developed and used in an effort to test the feasibility of a small-scale RDE. They used a single RDE with an outer diameter of 68mm, height of 50mm and a 4.5mm propellant injection channel width. This gives it about the dimensions of a paper cup. The design was also modular, as this would allow for lengthening of the chamber, or adding things like an aerospike (Dodd, 2020). In terms of results, the team did not look at thrust or Isp, but from the measurements of mass flow rate, and wavespeed, both can be calculated using the equations:(Specific impulse, 2021) and (Ishii et al., 2023).

In this paper, the mass flow rate is “45gs-1”,  and the speed of the wave is 18000m/s. Also, take the gross calorific value for hydrogen to be 141.7x106Jkg-1 (Editor Engineeringtoolbox, 2025). This calculated a thrust value of approximately 134N and an Isp of 304. These results show the practicality of small RDEs against a relative example - a model rocket motor using an APCP motor has an Isp of 207.55s (Hasan, 2025) and produces significantly less thrust for its size. Higher Isp in turn, means higher efficiency, so RDEs would be able to provide more thrust relative to weight class.

Case 2 Validation RDE (Bennewitz et al., 2023)

An RDE was designed and built using the “design guidelines of Bykovskii et al” (Bennewitz et al., 2023). Then tested by: Purdue University (Purdue), University of Central Florida (UCF), University of Washington (UW) and Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL). In these tests they investigated thrust and Isp relation to mass flow rate and they investigated wave speed, number of waves and pressure of chamber and propellants. 
The dimensions of the engine are 114mm in diameter to 108mm in height, roughly equal to 4410 cm3. In the test, the CTAP is a pressure sensor, and this was used to find internal pressure. In addition, cameras were used to find wavespeed, number of waves while load-cells were used in finding thrust. The difference was in how they angled and focused on their camera. The test also showed this design can operate well using cryogenic propellant and the chamber can handle the pressure as there no pressure spike suggesting a crack. Overview of Limitations and benefits

RDEs are more thermodynamically efficient, which allows them to be smaller and lighter than conventional rocket engines. Additionally, they can travel further on the same amount of propellant (Umarani, 2023, pp. 44-46). However, RDE technology has current drawbacks; for example, an experiment conducted by NASA found that Alloy 718 was not usable in the fuel injection plate. After a CT scan, the plate was found to be very eroded, with blobs of molten metal present in various places (Teasley et al., 2023). This is unfortunate, as GRcop 84 and 42 were the only alloys found to withstand the pressure in the RDE, but they cost $800 per kg compared to $200 per kg of Alloy 718, which is commonly used in aerospace and other industries. (‘GRCop-84 Powder: Unmatched Durability -Temperature Settings’, no date), (‘GRCop-42 Powder: Superior Alloy for Aerospace Industrial Use’, no date) , (‘Best inconel 718 powder for metal 3D printing 2024’, 2024) RDEs offer another advantage in theory. Although I have primarily discussed them as rocket engines, they are also used in tandem with other engines, like ramjets and scramjets (Assad et al., 2025). Finally, the last drawback of RDEs is that they have to be 3D printed, which increases the complexity and cost of the engine. This is because in RDEs, you have internal geometries which cannot be CNC as there is no way to get access to the section. Example of these is tesla valve used to prevent the backflow of propellant and the aerospike which need cooling channel as directs the detonation waves to allow for a more constant pressure (Liu et al., 2022). 





Conclusion

RDEs offer a way to improve are space and air travel capabilities in reducing the cost associated with fuel and time. From the analysis experiments, it is shown that we aren't far from having a viable RDE; the results show higher efficiency from smaller engines. For future work, more testing and research need to be conducted on larger RDEs and cheaper superalloys, as currently the main alloy GRcop is 4 times more expensive than standard aerospace materials.
